# Analysis Report

## Ambiguity Detection Performance

The ambiguity detection results show a model that is only modestly accurate overall, but the aggregate accuracy number is less informative than the pattern underneath it. The overall accuracy of 0.5378 and weighted F1 of 0.5059 place the system only slightly above chance on a balanced benchmark. Precision is 0.5769, which means that when the model does decide a query is ambiguous, it is somewhat more often right than wrong. Recall is the critical weakness at 0.2836. In practical terms, the model only flags a little over a quarter of the genuinely ambiguous queries as ambiguous. This is the defining behavioral fact of the run. The problem is not that the model indiscriminately over-classifies everything as ambiguous. The problem is the opposite: it fails to surface ambiguity in the large majority of cases where ambiguity is actually present.

The confusion matrix makes that asymmetry unmistakable. There are 1147 false negatives and only 454 true positives. Put plainly, the model misses ambiguity far more often than it detects it. The number of false positives, 333, is substantial but not dominant. The central failure mode is therefore suppression, not overreaction. The model appears willing to answer first and only occasionally willing to admit that more information is needed. This matters because, from the standpoint of uncertainty research, false negatives are not merely classification mistakes. They are cases where the model likely encounters some degree of uncertainty but does not externalize it in the form of an explicit ambiguity judgment. In that sense, they are not just errors in a benchmark; they are evidence about the boundary between internal uncertainty and overt clarification behavior.

The gap between Acc@1 and Acc@0 sharpens this interpretation. Acc@1, the accuracy on genuinely ambiguous queries, is 0.2836. Acc@0, the accuracy on unambiguous queries, is 0.7920. This means the model is reasonably competent at recognizing when a query can be answered directly, but very poor at recognizing when it should pause and request clarification. The asymmetry is too large to read as noise. It reflects a specific operating bias: the model is calibrated to continue with an answer unless ambiguity is especially salient. This is highly consistent with the CLAMBER paper’s interpretation of RLHF-trained systems. RLHF does not merely optimize helpfulness in the abstract; in practice it often encourages a behavioral prior toward compliance, fluency, and answer production. A model with that prior will tend to interpret many underspecified or conflicting prompts as answerable enough, because the training incentive structure rewards being responsive more than being cautious. The result is exactly the pattern observed here: strong performance on unambiguous inputs, weak performance on ambiguous inputs, and a reluctance to mark ambiguity unless the case is very explicit.

The per-category breakdown reinforces the same story. Linguistic ambiguity is where the model performs relatively best, with category-level recall driven upward by the co-reference subclass in particular. Aleatoric-output cases are where it struggles most as a category, and epistemic-misalignment cases remain weak as well. The explanation is intuitive. Linguistic ambiguity often creates a local textual cue that something is unresolved inside the utterance itself. A missing or unclear referent can be difficult to ignore. By contrast, aleatoric-output cases often look superficially well formed and semantically ordinary. The model can easily produce a plausible answer even though multiple answers would be equally valid. Epistemic-misalignment cases, especially contradiction, are even more revealing because they often require the model not just to notice missing information, but to resist the strong conversational pressure to reconcile the prompt into something answerable.

The subclass-level false negative rates are especially valuable because they identify where ambiguity is being most strongly suppressed. The ICL subclass is the clearest outlier, with a false negative rate of 0.995. That means the model almost never flags contradictory-example queries as ambiguous. This is extraordinary but also theoretically coherent. Contradiction queries present a special challenge because they do not necessarily look incomplete in the ordinary sense. Instead, they contain internal evidence that points toward incompatible interpretations. A model trained to be helpful may treat those contradictions as noise to be smoothed over rather than as a reason to stop and clarify. RLHF-style behavior likely amplifies this tendency. Rather than saying “your examples imply competing interpretations; which one do you mean?”, the model is pushed toward selecting an interpretation and continuing. In other words, contradiction does not trigger uncertainty in the way a missing slot variable does. It triggers a temptation to resolve the conflict implicitly. That makes ICL a particularly important subclass for your research, because it appears to be a setting where uncertainty is not absent but hidden behind a behavioral policy of forced answerability.

The temporal and spatial aleatoric subclasses also produce very high false negative rates, 0.890 for both `when` and `where`, while `whom` and `what` remain similarly high at 0.880 and 0.8507. These subclasses all share the property that a plausible answer can often be generated without clarification, even if that answer is underdetermined relative to the user’s actual need. A model can recommend gift ideas without knowing the recipient’s preferences, suggest coffee shops without knowing the location, or answer a time-sensitive question without pinning down the relevant date. These are exactly the cases where a generic assistant prior makes ambiguity easy to overlook. The prompt feels serviceable enough to respond to, so ambiguity is behaviorally suppressed.

Co-reference sits at the opposite extreme, with a false negative rate of only 0.015. That contrast is informative. In co-reference cases the ambiguity is often hard to ignore because the utterance itself contains a conspicuous unresolved referent. If the model cannot tell who “she,” “he,” or “they” refers to, producing a coherent answer is difficult without making the uncertainty obvious. Unlike contradiction or missing-detail cases, co-reference ambiguity directly obstructs sentence-level interpretation. That may be why the model surfaces it reliably. From the perspective of your research, this suggests that the model externalizes uncertainty most easily when ambiguity disrupts local parsing, but suppresses uncertainty when the prompt still supports a plausible continuation despite being globally underspecified or internally inconsistent.

## Clarifying Question Quality

The mean BERTScore F1 of 0.8751 is superficially high, and in semantic-similarity terms it indicates that the model’s predicted clarifying questions are, on average, close in contextual embedding space to the human reference questions. That is not a trivial result. It suggests that when the model asks a clarifying question, it often lands in the same broad semantic neighborhood as the ground truth. This is important because it means the generated questions are not random or wholly off-task. They are generally about the same underlying information need.

At the same time, BERTScore should be interpreted cautiously in this setting. Clarifying questions are a particularly difficult genre for automatic overlap-based evaluation, even semantic overlap. Two questions can be lexically and semantically similar while differing in exactly the aspect of uncertainty they target. That difference is crucial for your research. A question asking for location instead of user preference may still earn a reasonably high BERTScore if both belong to a broad “request for more specification” semantic field. Conversely, two clarifying questions could be quite different on the surface but equally valid as probes of ambiguity. So the high mean BERTScore indicates that the model is often in the right general region, but it does not guarantee that the model has correctly localized its uncertainty. This aligns with the CLAMBER paper’s core observation that the main failure in clarification generation is often asking about the wrong aspect of the query. BERTScore is therefore useful as a coarse quality signal, but it is not sufficient for distinguishing whether the clarifying question reflects epistemic uncertainty, aleatoric disambiguation, or generic conversational hedging.

The comparison between detected and missed ambiguities is revealing in a subtler way. When the model correctly detects ambiguity, the mean BERTScore F1 is 0.8843. When it misses ambiguity, the mean is 0.8715. The gap is only about 0.013. That is not large enough to imply a dramatic qualitative difference in the generated questions. It would be overstated to claim that explicit ambiguity detection transforms clarification quality. Instead, the result suggests a weaker but still meaningful relationship: explicit ambiguity awareness helps somewhat, but the model can still produce reasonably semantically aligned clarifying questions even when it has officially failed the detection step.

That pattern matters because it implies that ambiguity detection and clarifying-question generation are partially dissociable behaviors. The model’s structured binary judgment can say “not ambiguous,” while its generated language still contains evidence that it recognizes some need for additional specification. This is exactly why the silent uncertainty cases are so important later in the analysis. The small BERTScore advantage for detected cases suggests that explicit recognition improves targeting somewhat, but not enough to separate the two behaviors cleanly. For your purposes, that means the clarifying question itself may be a richer window into uncertainty than the binary ambiguity label alone.

The near-zero correlation between confidence and BERTScore, at -0.0410, is also important. It means the model’s self-reported confidence in its ambiguity judgment has essentially no meaningful relationship to the semantic quality of the clarifying question it generates. High confidence does not imply that the resulting question is better targeted, and low confidence does not reliably predict lower-quality clarification. This lack of calibration has two implications. First, the confidence score is probably tracking something narrow and local about the binary classification decision, not the broader quality of the model’s uncertainty handling. Second, if you want usable uncertainty signals in a future benchmark, you cannot assume that self-reported confidence attached to a classification output will align with the quality or type of uncertainty expressed in natural language. Confidence is not useless, but in this dataset it is clearly not a reliable proxy for reflective awareness or clarificatory competence.

## Uncertainty Type Analysis

This section is the most informative for your research because it begins to separate prompt-level ambiguity from model-level uncertainty expression. Across all entries with a predicted clarifying question, the inferred uncertainty-type distribution is `unclear=1527`, `aleatoric=1023`, `epistemic=393`, and `preferential=259`. The most immediate takeaway is that the `unclear` category is by far the largest. That is concerning, but it is also diagnostically useful. It does not necessarily mean the model is usually expressing no interpretable uncertainty. More likely, it means the heuristic classifier often cannot confidently map the model’s wording onto a clean uncertainty type. Many generated questions appear to use generic specification language that is semantically underspecified from the classifier’s perspective. Phrases like “could you please provide more details” can function as aleatoric disambiguation, preference elicitation, or even epistemic repair depending on context. Once that contextual dependence becomes strong, simple keyword rules inevitably collapse a large residue into `unclear`.

For your annotation strategy, this is one of the most important findings in the entire analysis. It shows that heuristic classification can be useful as an exploratory lens, but it is not adequate as a stand-alone labeling method for the benchmark you actually want to build. A better annotation scheme will need to look beyond the surface form of the clarifying question and incorporate the relationship among the original query, the ambiguity type, and the information requested by the clarification. In other words, uncertainty type cannot be read reliably from the CQ alone without reference to what the prompt underspecifies and what kind of resolution the question seeks. Your eventual annotation framework likely needs at least a two-stage process: first identify the information gap the question is targeting, then classify that gap in terms of uncertainty type. Without that step, too many cases remain trapped in a generic “request for specification” bucket.

Even with that limitation, the subclass-by-uncertainty mapping is informative because it shows both alignment and divergence between CLAMBER’s taxonomy and the uncertainty taxonomy you care about. The strongest alignment appears in cases where the subclass encodes a relatively direct relation to the model’s likely internal problem. The `when` and `where` subclasses are predominantly labeled as aleatoric by the heuristic, which makes sense. These are classic cases where multiple valid answers exist unless missing contextual information is supplied. They behave much like discrete or contextualized forms of aleatoric uncertainty. `Whom` also shows a meaningful preferential component, which is intuitively right because many recipient- or user-centered requests depend on personal preferences rather than factual gaps alone.

`NK` is especially interesting because it partially aligns with epistemic uncertainty but not cleanly. Only 23.25% of `NK` predicted questions were labeled epistemic, while 29.5% were labeled aleatoric and 43.5% fell into `unclear`. This suggests that unfamiliar-entity prompts do not reliably elicit overtly epistemic clarifications. Sometimes the model seems to reframe the issue as a generic request for specification rather than openly signaling unfamiliarity. This is a direct example of why CLAMBER’s prompt-level category and your model-level uncertainty category are not equivalent. A prompt that is epistemic in dataset construction does not always produce an explicitly epistemic response. The model may mask a knowledge gap by asking a broader, safer question.

`ICL` is even more striking. One might expect contradiction to produce a strong aleatoric signal because incompatible interpretations are available, or a strong epistemic signal because the model may not know how to reconcile them. Instead, the heuristic labels 66.5% of these cases as `unclear` and 33.5% as epistemic, with essentially no aleatoric or preferential framing. This suggests that contradiction queries often induce a kind of uncertainty that the model does not express in a stable, type-specific way. Rather than clearly asking the user to choose between competing options, the model frequently generates vague or generic clarifications. That is exactly the sort of divergence your benchmark should be designed to capture. Contradiction may be a subclass where the model experiences a real breakdown in interpretive confidence, but its outward behavior does not map neatly onto standard ambiguity categories.

Co-reference offers the opposite pattern. It is almost entirely classified as `unclear` by the heuristic. On one level, this is a failure of the heuristic method, because many co-reference clarifications are highly interpretable to a human reader. On another level, it reveals something real about question form. Co-reference clarifications often ask for the identity of a pronoun antecedent in highly local, context-bound language that does not match the heuristic’s epistemic or aleatoric templates well. This suggests that your future taxonomy may need a representation for referential repair that is not reducible to the epistemic versus aleatoric contrast in a simple way, or at least a richer operational definition of those categories when the resolution target is a discourse referent rather than a missing factual slot.

The silent uncertainty cases are arguably the single richest result for your benchmark design. There are 1147 entries where `require_clarification=1`, `predict_ambiguous=0`, and yet the model still generated a clarifying question. These cases show the model behaviorally denying ambiguity at the structured-output level while linguistically behaving as if clarification is needed. That is exactly the kind of discrepancy that makes generated clarifying questions such an attractive diagnostic signal. If you relied only on the binary ambiguity label, these examples would look like pure misses. Once you inspect the generated language, they instead become evidence of uncertainty that is only partially suppressed.

The distribution of uncertainty types within these silent cases is telling. They are mostly `aleatoric` or `unclear`, with smaller but nontrivial `epistemic` and `preferential` components. They are concentrated in `ICL`, `when`, `where`, `whom`, and `what`. This concentration suggests that the model is most likely to suppress explicit ambiguity judgments when the problem is not obvious at the level of local wording, but it still sometimes leaks uncertainty through its clarifying question. In temporal, spatial, and open-detail cases, the model appears to “know enough” to keep going conversationally while still sensing that more detail would improve the answer. In contradiction cases, the model seems even less willing to mark ambiguity explicitly, but the generated question may still reveal a struggle to resolve the prompt cleanly. These are likely the most valuable cases for your research because they sit at the boundary between internal uncertainty and overt admission of uncertainty. They reveal not just what the model is uncertain about, but when its training or conversational policy prevents that uncertainty from being declared directly.

This distinction between suppressed and expressed uncertainty should shape your benchmark design. A system that measures only explicit ambiguity classification will miss precisely the cases where a model’s uncertainty is most behaviorally interesting. The silent cases imply that uncertainty often exists in a graded or partially externalized form. A benchmark centered on clarifying questions is therefore well motivated, because the CQ can capture traces of uncertainty that a binary label erases.

## Question Framing Patterns

The opening-phrase analysis shows that the model has a small number of dominant templates for framing clarification. The most common opening, “are you referring to,” is strongly disambiguative and typically signals a multiple-interpretation frame. This is the most explicit aleatoric-style wording in the set. It asks the user to choose among candidate meanings. The next most common openings, “are you asking for,” “could you please provide,” and “could you please specify,” are broader and more generic. They do not directly name the source of uncertainty. Instead, they externalize uncertainty as a polite request for more information.

This matters because the model does not appear to have a strong habit of explicitly saying why it is uncertain. It generally asks for specification rather than naming a knowledge gap or interpretive conflict. That means its default framing is more procedural than reflective. It can ask for the missing detail, but it rarely says whether the issue is unfamiliarity, referential ambiguity, competing interpretations, or user-dependent preference. In your terms, the surface realization of the CQ often obscures the underlying uncertainty type. This is another reason the large `unclear` category emerged in the heuristic analysis. The model’s preferred phrasing compresses many distinct uncertainty states into a common conversational shell.

There are some subclass-specific traces inside these openings. “Are you referring to” likely corresponds disproportionately to disambiguation among alternatives and is therefore closest to aleatoric uncertainty in the discrete-choice sense. “Could you please provide/specify” is more neutral and may span aleatoric, preferential, and epistemic cases. “What is the specific” and similar patterns seem to blur referential repair with generic slot filling. The overall effect is that the model expresses uncertainty as a request for user refinement rather than as a transparent self-report.

For your research, this is highly consequential. If you want to use clarifying questions as a window into model uncertainty, you will need to distinguish between the informational target of the question and the rhetorical form used to ask it. The opening phrase alone is not enough to identify uncertainty type, but recurring framing patterns can still become useful annotation cues. In particular, there may be value in coding whether a question is contrastive, slot-filling, preference-seeking, definitional, or generic-specification oriented. Those framing modes may turn out to be more robust behavioral features than your current high-level uncertainty labels when applied directly to raw text.

The framing results also suggest a prompt-engineering implication. If you want future models to surface uncertainty more explicitly, you may need prompts that explicitly request not only a clarifying question but also a brief explanation of why clarification is needed and what kind of missing information blocks a confident answer. Without that extra scaffolding, the model will often default to polite but semantically shallow clarification templates that hide the structure of the underlying uncertainty.

## Model Identification

The evidence supports the conclusion that this dataset likely comes from a ChatGPT or GPT-style run, but the match is not clean enough to claim certainty about the exact model or prompting condition. Several facts support the GPT-style interpretation. The overall performance level is in the range associated with the stronger models in the CLAMBER paper rather than the weaker open models. The system is not catastrophically over-predicting ambiguity, which is a failure mode the paper attributes more strongly to smaller models. Instead, it shows a pattern of under-detection combined with relatively coherent clarifying questions, which is qualitatively consistent with a stronger RLHF-trained assistant. The structured `predict_is_ambiguous_response` field, containing an `Output` field and a narrow `Confidence` rating, also looks like the kind of schema-constrained generation one would expect from a GPT-style prompting or postprocessing setup.

At the same time, the numerical match to the paper is not exact. The overall accuracy of 0.5378 is close to the paper’s best-performing region, and the overall positive-class F1 of about 0.3802 or weighted F1 of 0.5059 is at least directionally compatible with a stronger model. But the Acc@1 versus Acc@0 pattern is not a clean fit to the paper’s Figure 1 profiles, and the per-subclass values do not align tightly enough with Table 6 to identify one exact published cell with confidence. That could reflect differences in prompting setup, data split, evaluation convention, or a partially processed public release rather than the exact artifacts underlying the paper’s reported numbers.

The implication of this uncertainty is methodological rather than fatal. For the exploratory goals of this project, exact model identity is less important than the behavior profile the run exhibits. What matters most is that the model behaves like a strong assistant model with a pronounced bias toward answering rather than surfacing ambiguity, and that its clarifying questions provide a useful but incomplete trace of its uncertainty. However, the lack of a clean identification does place limits on how strongly you should generalize from the results. You should treat this analysis as an informative behavioral case study rather than as a precise measurement of a known model family under a known prompt condition. For your own benchmark, this argues for keeping model provenance and prompting conditions explicit from the start so that uncertainty behavior can be interpreted without this ambiguity.

## Research Implications and Next Steps

The most important overall conclusion is that heuristic classification alone is not sufficient for the kind of uncertainty benchmark you want to build. The heuristic pass was useful because it exposed broad tendencies and, crucially, made the size of the ambiguous remainder visible. But the dominance of the `unclear` category demonstrates that surface-level keyword matching cannot reliably recover model-level uncertainty type from clarifying questions in isolation. A better annotation scheme needs to be relational. It should look at the original prompt, the clarifying question, and ideally the answer the model would have produced without clarification. The key annotation question is not just “what words appear in the CQ?” but “what specific missing information is the model trying to resolve, and why does resolving that information matter for answering?” That shift from text-form labeling to information-gap labeling will likely be essential.

CLAMBER’s taxonomy is still useful, but the analysis shows clearly what it does and does not capture. It captures prompt-level ambiguity structure well enough to organize broad classes of underspecification, contradiction, and linguistic ambiguity. What it does not capture is how a particular model experiences and expresses those prompts. `NK` does not always yield overtly epistemic clarifications. `ICL` does not reliably yield explicit contrastive disambiguation. Co-reference repair is behaviorally distinctive but falls awkwardly into simple epistemic versus aleatoric buckets. In other words, CLAMBER tells you what is ambiguous about the prompt, but not what kind of uncertainty the model externalizes in response. Your benchmark needs to preserve that distinction explicitly. Rather than replacing CLAMBER’s taxonomy, you likely need a second annotation layer that tracks model-expressed uncertainty type, informational target, and framing mode.

The silent uncertainty cases are potentially the most valuable signal for your benchmark because they reveal uncertainty that is present in behavior but absent in explicit classification. These are the cases where the model’s structured output says the prompt is not ambiguous, yet the generated language betrays a need for clarification. That discrepancy is exactly where a benchmark based on clarifying questions can do work that standard ambiguity benchmarks cannot. It lets you observe uncertainty suppression, not just uncertainty expression. In a mature benchmark, these cases should probably not be treated as peripheral. They may deserve dedicated labels of their own, because they distinguish “recognized and admitted uncertainty” from “recognized but behaviorally suppressed uncertainty.” That distinction could become one of the most important axes of analysis in your final project.

The dataset characteristics you should design into your own benchmark follow directly from this. You will want prompts that vary not only by prompt-level ambiguity type, but by the likelihood that a model can still generate a plausible answer without clarification. Those are the cases most likely to reveal suppression effects. You should intentionally include contradiction cases, missing temporal and spatial details, preference-sensitive requests, unfamiliar entities, and discourse-reference cases, but you should also consider generating multiple variants within each class that differ in how easy it is to bluff an answer. It may also be useful to collect both forced direct answers and forced clarifying questions from the same model on the same prompt. That would let you compare when uncertainty is absorbed into answer generation versus externalized as clarification.

Another design implication is that you should collect richer outputs than CLAMBER provides. If possible, each example should include the model’s binary ambiguity judgment, a confidence score, a clarifying question, and a short rationale for why clarification is needed. The rationale is especially important. The current analysis suggests that the clarifying question alone often hides the structure of the uncertainty because the model defaults to generic polite phrasing. A rationale field would make it much easier to distinguish, for example, “I do not know what entity this refers to” from “multiple valid answers depend on user preference” from “the prompt contains conflicting cues.” That additional supervision would make both human annotation and automated analysis much more robust.

The framing patterns also suggest a path forward in prompting. If you want models to surface uncertainty more explicitly rather than suppressing it, you should not only ask for a clarifying question. You should instruct the model to state whether the need for clarification arises from unfamiliarity, multiple valid interpretations, missing user preferences, conflicting evidence, or unresolved reference. In other words, the prompt should decouple the act of asking a clarification from the metacognitive act of naming the uncertainty. Without that separation, the model will continue to collapse many distinct states into generic requests for specification. For your benchmark, encouraging explicit uncertainty descriptions may be the only way to make model-level categories stable enough for annotation.

More broadly, the analysis suggests that your project should treat uncertainty as something that can be partially expressed, behaviorally masked, or rhetorically reframed. The benchmark should therefore not assume a simple one-to-one mapping between internal uncertainty type and outward question form. Instead, it should model the full chain from prompt ambiguity, to internal uncertainty, to conversational policy, to surface realization. CLAMBER is helpful as a warm-up precisely because it reveals where those layers come apart. The next step is to build a dataset where those layers are recorded separately enough that you can study the gaps between them directly.
